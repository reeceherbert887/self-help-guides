# What Pepper Is

Pepper is a humanoid social robot developed by SoftBank Robotics and first introduced in 2014. It was designed to interact naturally with humans and recognise emotions through voice, facial expressions, and body language.

Unlike traditional industrial robots that focus on manufacturing tasks, Pepper was built specifically for human interaction. It is commonly used in environments such as education, research laboratories, retail stores, and public spaces where robots interact directly with people.

# Key characteristics include:

- A humanoid robot design that enables natural interaction with people

- A tablet display on the chest used for information, interaction, and applications

- Multiple sensors and cameras to detect people and understand the surrounding environment

- Designed to communicate with humans through speech, gestures, and visual interaction

Although originally designed for commercial use in customer service and hospitality, Pepper is now widely used in education and research.

# How Pepper Works

Pepper works through a combination of sensors, motors, and software systems that allow it to perceive people and interact socially.

Mechanical system

- Pepper has multiple joints in the head, arms, and torso, allowing it to perform expressive gestures and movements.

- The robot moves using a wheeled base that enables smooth and stable navigation.

Sensors and perception

- Pepper includes several sensing systems:

- Cameras in the head for face detection and object recognition

- Microphones to detect and process human speech

- Sonar and infrared sensors to detect nearby obstacles

- Touch sensors in the head and hands to detect human interaction

These sensors allow Pepper to:

- Detect and recognise people

- Interpret speech and basic emotions

- Navigate safely around obstacles

This allows Pepper to function as a social robot designed for communication and interaction.

Programming and control

Pepper is designed to support both graphical programming and software development.

It can be programmed in two main ways:

1. Graphical programming (Choregraphe)

- Developers create behaviours using a visual programming interface

- Actions are built using connected behaviour boxes

- Pepper performs sequences of speech, movement, and interaction

2. Software programming

- Uses the NAOqi software framework

- Developers can write code using Python, C++, or JavaScript

- Programs control movement, sensors, speech, and interactive behaviours

# Why Pepper Is Important in Robotics

- Pepper was significant because it introduced several key ideas in social robotics:

- Human-robot interaction through speech and gestures

- Emotion recognition and social communication

- Service robotics for public environments

- Robots designed specifically for interaction rather than manufacturing

These ideas contributed to the development of modern social robots used in education, healthcare, and customer service.

Pepper at the University of Hull

The University of Hull robotics laboratories include Pepper alongside other robots such as NAO, Baxter, and TurtleBot3. Students use these platforms to explore robotics, artificial intelligence, and human-robot interaction.

In practice, Pepper at Hull is typically used for:

1. Robotics programming

Students learn to program Pepper using tools such as:

- Choregraphe visual programming software

- Python programming through the NAOqi framework

- Robot behaviour design and interaction systems

This helps students understand robot behaviour design, human interaction systems, and AI-driven communication.

2. Human-robot interaction experiments

Because Pepper is designed for social interaction, it is useful for experiments involving:

- Speech-based interaction

- Gesture-based communication

- Interactive demonstrations and presentations

3. Research and project work

Pepper can be used for projects such as:

- Interactive games and demonstrations

- Voice recognition experiments

- AI-driven conversation systems

- Social robotics research

4. Teaching robotics fundamentals

- Students use Pepper to learn:

- Human-robot interaction design

- Sensor integration and perception

- Behaviour programming

- Social robotics systems
